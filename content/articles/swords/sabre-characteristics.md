# Sabre Characteristics

Weapon characteristics are where things get really hyper-localized. When it comes to protective equipment, you could argue that a lot of the guides are a bit generic. I've written this based on the gear I see in my local scene, but otherwise Thokk gloves are just as good here as they are across the pond.

It's a bit different for swords. The "meta" in Southern Ontario tends towards light-to-medium weight sabres, usually with cuphilts, and usually with partial curve blades. The exception is broadswords, which are technically "heavier" by virtue of their baskethilt, and yet can be just as nimble as a medium weight sabre thanks to being so backweighted.

What this all means for you:

## Tournament Specifications

If you plan to attend tournaments, aim for a sabre that weighs 700g at minimum, and has a maximum blade length of 87cm (approx 34"). These are the requirements set by one of the main tournament organizers in the region, so you'll need to work inside these limits to pass gear check.

Tournament rules change farther afield. For example: Montreal Sword Meisters sets the minimum weight at 600g, and Maritime Sword School at 750g.

## Non-Tournament Specifications

If you don't plan on attending a tournament, then good news: you get to pick whatever sabre works best for you. Blade lengths tend to all be pretty samey which means the main thing you're looking for is a sword that speaks to you.

## Weight

All else being equal, lighter sabres are more nimble, quicker to change direction, and gentler on our opponents. Light sabres give up presence however; a heavier sabre can blow through an imperfect guard performed by a light sabre, and a heavy sabre guarded imperfectly can sometimes still have enough mass to deflect a blow.

The sabre's weight is not strictly about its performance. Light sabres are less energy demanding, letting you drill and fence longer before tiring out. Heavy sabres can tire out the wielder more quickly, though with the practice and the right actions, heavy sabres can abuse properties of physics to let gravity do a lot of the work.

Which one is best for you? Try some and decide. In general though: a light sabre is terrifying in the hands of someone who is themselves fast and sneaky. A heavy sabre is terrifying in the hands of someone who has a lock on distance and knows how to control the tempo.

Our club considers "duelling" sabres to be in the **600-700g range**. Something weighing at least 700g will be required to get into most Ontario tournaments (though some accept lighter). Duelling sabres are tons of fun, and you can absolutely use these in class regardless of what's allowed in tournaments.

Our club considers "light-to-medium" sabres to be in the **700-800g** range. The forward/backward balance of swords in this weight class start to make a big difference, and ideally compliment your fencing style.

Our club considers medium-heavy sabres to be in the **800-950g** range. These have a lot of presence and can parry a dueling sabre even if the strong/weak placement isn't perfect. While I wouldn't strictly recommend against a sabre in the upper range, I will say that a 900g sabre will likely put you at a disadvantage as a new fencer.

A broadsword usually starts at **1kg** with most of this weight concentrated in the baskethilt. A 1kg broadsword tends to perform much more like a 700-800g sabre, but with better presence because that mass still exists even if it is backweighted. One of our fencers described broadswords as "they parry for you."

An important consideration is that not all swords weigh their advertised weight. There is a lot of manual process that goes into their creation which can result in discrepancies. If you're ordering from a distributor, you can ask them to weigh a sword before you buy it. If you're ordering from an OEM, you can try to ask for more information, or in some cases request a specific weight (see the customization section below).

## Balance

Is the sabre forward weighted, or back weighted? The balance can make a heavy sabre feel more nimble, or a light sabre feel like it wants to "go." OEMs will list this specification as its "point of balance," which is the spot measured from the guard where the sword can balance itself if placed on your finger.

A forward weighted sabre really wants to go, and works well with moulinets. A forward weighted sabre will have more presence than a backweighted sabre of the same weight.

A backweighted sabre will feel more nimble, and will depend on you to make effective cuts and thrusts. A backweighted sabre will have less presence than a forward weighted sabre of the same weight.

## Cup/Hilt Style

HEMA sabres commonly have a cuphilt to (partially) hide your fingers behind. This affords good protection, brings some of the weight backwards, and is often ambidexterous.

A **three bar** hilt is an ornamental design that looks fantastic at the expense of slightly less protection. Perhaps more importantly: three bar designs tend to be either right- or left-handed, so if you are getting a three bar sabre, make sure it matches your handedness.

A **stirrup hilt** or **knuckle bow** can offer you a lot of mobility, or room for truly massive gloves at the expense of reduced hand protection (though there are some historical designs to mitigate this).

A **pecoraro hilt** resembles a cuphilt with "ears" on either side which can provide more coverage for the thumb. This extra protection can prevent some sizes of medium or hardshell gloves from fitting inside.

A **baskethilt** is an (often elaborate) hilt that wraps around the entire hand. These provide excellent defence against cuts, and can allow you to wear a lighter glove.

## Blade Curvature

Sabre blades can be straight, partially curved, or fully curved. Some curvature is useful for shedding techniques, and full curve can be fun for certain kinds of sneaky thrusts. While I'm sure one of these is mathematically more optimized than the other, it is for the most part a preference that depends on how you like to fence.

Some sabre makers offer multiple levels of curvature on all swords, and some offer only one.

All broadswords have straight blades.

## Tips

You'll find three kinds of tips on most sabres:

**Round tips** are to be avoided whenever possible. This is a trainer with a tip that ends in a semi-circle instead of a point. These will wear down leather tips somewhat quickly, and it can be unpleasant (or even dangerous) to receive a thrust from an untipped round tip. Thermoplastic tips can strongly mitigate a round tip if you find a sword you want that only comes with round tips; otherwise, prefer a rolled or spatulated tip.

**Rolled tips** are offer the most surface area and therefore the most safety _while intact_. The tip of the blade has been folded in on itself to make a nice big round surface. Rolled tips may be slightly weaker and more prone to breaking, which is why tipping it with something bright is important to help notice a broken blade. That's not to say they are fragile -- they can and do last a long time.

**Spatulated tips** are like a reverse sword tip. Instead of the blade focusing into a thin pinpoint, it flares outwards to create a larger surface area. Spatulated tips _can_ offer good protection if the blade has a large profile to begin with, but a spatulated tip does not guarantee safety, and still must be tipped. I remember someone describing a low-surface area spatulated rapier tip as being "unrecognizable from a leather punch" after it was involved in an puncturing incident. Regardless, even the worst spatulated tip provides an excellent surface for attaching a leather or thermoplastic tip.

## Customization

Because many sabres are made-to-order, you can sometimes request customizations. Some of these are offered formarlly as part of the ordering process like engravings or wrap materials and colours, and some of these you may have to reach out and ask about. Some shops will allow you to request changes to the weight or blade length for an additional charge, though I have heard of at least one sword maker who would not offer a warranty on such alterations.

Such "unlisted" customizations are not guaranteed, though as long as you're polite, it doesn't hurt to ask. If you're not polite, bear in mind these are small businesses and you may be talking to the person who will be making your sword, maybe even the owner. Sword makers have plenty enough demand to ignore your order if they decide it's not worth the hassle.
