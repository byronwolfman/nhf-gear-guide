# Longsword Characteristics

Weapon characteristics are where things get really hyper-localized. When it comes to protective equipment, you could argue that a lot of the guides are a bit generic. I've written this based on the gear I see in my local scene, but otherwise Thokk gloves are just as good here as they are across the pond.

It’s a bit different for swords. The “meta” in Southern Ontario tends towards agile, quick feders. Exchanges are quick and binds almost always lead to a scoring event. The next few years could be interesting too, now that there are a bunch of Esker Forge longswords floating around. These are fast swords that weigh only ~1360g and yet bind like a much heavier sword. While Esker Forge longswords aren't being produced anymore, I'm hopeful that someone will pick up their design lessons and move towards similarly lighter (which is to say _safer_) longswords.

What this all means for you:

## Tournament Specifications

Unlike sabres which have weight minimums, longswords usually have maximums. If you plan to attend tournaments, your upper limit is a 1800g (though I'd recommend aiming for much lower), and the blade length should be no more than 104cm.

Tournament rules change farther afield. For example: Montreal Sword Meisters sets the maximum weight at 1700g, and the Maritime Sword School at 1650. Considering the popularity of Montreal Sword Meisters (and therefore its ability to influence other rulesets), you may wish to set your upper limit at 1700g regardless. That's a pretty heavy sword even without rules saying so, and probably heavier than what most beginners should get.

## Non-Tournament Specifications

If you don't plan on attending a tournament, then good news: you get to pick whatever longsword works best for you.

Blade lengths can vary quite a bit, with some being as short as 87cm (approx 34") or as long as 104cm (approx 41"). Anything shorter than 95cm may put you at a disadvantage, and on the other hand, swords longer than 101cm can make certain techniques harder. Sure, longer blades are great when you and your opponent decide to thrust at the same time, but longer blades also need more room to disengage, giving your opponent better binding and winding opportunities.

I wouldn't agonize too much over the exact length. Try some longswords, see what works for you, and then go buy a longsword of a similar length, give or take a few cm.

## Weight

Even a heavy longsword can move pretty fast on a first intention. Lighter longswords can change directions faster which can be useful after initial contact. Heavy longswords on the other hand tend to be associated with stronger binds, owing to there being more material in the blade. [Esker Forge would disagree](https://eskerforge.com/principles-of-sword-design/) because their swords bind through carefully tuned physics rather than mass alone, but their swords are (so far) exceptional.

Our club considers "light" longswords to be in the **1250-1400g** range. These are fun and you can get in a lot of reps. When striking into opposition, a lighter longsword (all other things being equal) will have less presence.

Our club considers "medium" longswords to be in the **1400-1550g** range. These have presence without being overly unkind to your opponent's health. A lot of popular longswords fall into this range.

Our club considers "heavy" longswords to be anything over **1550g**. The added mass helps with parrying and works well with blade actions, but these swords can also be tiring to use without conditioning. Control becomes especially important with heavier longswords so that we do not injure our opponent.

An important consideration is that not all swords weigh their advertised weight. There is a lot of manual process that goes into their creation which can result in discrepancies. If you’re ordering from a distributor, you can ask them to weigh a sword before you buy it. If you’re ordering from an OEM, you can try to ask for more information, or in some cases request a specific weight (see the customization section below).

## Balance

Is the longsword forward weighted, or back weighted? The balance can make a heavy longsword feel more nimble, or a light longsword feel like it wants to "go." OEMs will list this specification as its “point of balance,” which is the spot measured from the guard where the sword can balance itself if placed on your finger.

A forward weighted longsword really wants to go, and can strike well in opposition. Beats with a forward weighted longsword can transfer a lot of momentum into an opponent's sword, and more easily get it offline.

A backweighted longsword will feel more nimble, and will depend on you to make effective cuts and thrusts. While beats, parries, and bind work alwyas works better when using your strong against an opponent's weak, this will matter more with a backweighted longsword.

## Crossguard Styles

Though nominally for defence, a crossguard's quillons are also a great way to make your sword stand out or be unique. It's therefore important to understand how this affects safety.

Squared and bulbed ends are safest. Curved/upswept quillons might look like they would catch an opposing sword better, but in practice make little difference. Curved/upswept quillons can be unsafe for our opponent though. Some crossguards include hand protection rings, which are controversial in a sometimes funny way. As a beginner, err on getting a longsword _without_ crossguard rings. We have no self-control and rarely stop at one sword, so if you really want crossguard rings, you can always get them on your next sword.

Overall: prefer crossguard styles that have blunt or rounded faces, and which do not otherwise change the overall geometry of the longsword.

## Pommels

The way we use our offhand lets us use a longsword like a lever instead of a hammer. An OEM that lets you customize the pommel is letting you pick something that looks cool and also changes the sword's feel.

Pear pommels get the job done, but depending on size and gloves, may sometimes let your hands slip. Onion, mushroom, and faceted pommels share the basic idea behind a pear pommel while offering better retention.

Disc pommels are another option that help with edge alignment since discs have distinct faces. Alignment arguably comes with time rather than pommel shape, but swords are deeply personal so if you have the chance to try one, see if it speaks to you.

Note any weight differences when choosing a pommel, as these will moderately shift the sword's weight. "Moderately" is the operative word though unless you are chosing a truly massive pommel.

## Blade Types

There are, broadly speaking, two types of blades: longsword blades, and feder blades.

**Longsword blades** look like the real thing; they begin wide at the hilt, and taper as they reach the point. This puts a lot of the sword's mass in front of the hilt, which can give it more forward weight, and transfers more force with strikes.

**Feder blades** (short for "federschwert" or "feather sword") often have a few inches near the hilt where their width is that of a normal longsword called a "schilt," and then thin considerably for the rest of the length of the blade. The lighter blade is kinder to opponents and transfers some of the weight backwards.

Feder blades are a better option for training most of the time, being both more forgiving to you and your opponent. There are reasons to get a longsword blade, so if you're unsure, I would recommend a feder as your default option. Remember: it's your first sword, not your only sword!

## Tips

You'll find three kinds of tips on most longswords:

**Round tips** are to be avoided whenever possible. This is a trainer with a tip that ends in a semi-circle instead of a point. These will wear down leather tips somewhat quickly, and it can be unpleasant (or even dangerous) to receive a thrust from an untipped round tip. Thermoplastic tips can strongly mitigate a round tip if you find a sword you want that only comes with round tips; otherwise, prefer a rolled or spatulated tip.

**Rolled tips** are offer the most surface area and therefore the most safety _while intact_. The tip of the blade has been folded in on itself to make a nice big round surface. Rolled tips may be slightly weaker and more prone to breaking, which is why tipping it with something bright is important to help notice a broken blade. That's not to say they are fragile -- they can and do last a long time.

**Spatulated tips** are like a reverse sword tip. Instead of the blade focusing into a thin pinpoint, it flares outwards to create a larger surface area. Spatulated tips _can_ offer good protection if the blade has a large profile to begin with, but a spatulated tip does not guarantee safety, and still must be tipped. I remember someone describing a low-surface area spatulated rapier tip as being "unrecognizable from a leather punch" after it was involved in an puncturing incident. Regardless, even the worst spatulated tip provides an excellent surface for attaching a leather or thermoplastic tip.

## Customization

Because many longswords are made-to-order, you can sometimes request customizations. Some of these are offered formally as part of the ordering process like engravings or wrap materials and colours, and some of these you may have to reach out and ask about. Some shops will allow you to request changes to the weight or blade length for an additional charge, though I have heard of at least one sword maker who would not offer a warranty on such alterations.

Such "unlisted" customizations are not guaranteed, though as long as you're polite, it doesn't hurt to ask. If you're not polite, bear in mind these are small businesses and you may be talking to the person who will be making your sword, maybe even even the owner. Sword makers have plenty enough demand to ignore your order if they decide it's not worth the hassle.
