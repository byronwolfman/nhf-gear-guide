# Tipping

Nominally blunt tips become sharp when steel trainers break. This does not happen frequently, and a well-maintained trainer without any manufacturing defects can last for years. It doesn't happen _never_ though, especially when we keep launching them at each other, so we must all take measures to stay safe when the inevitable happens.

Tipping is mandatory at NHF and serves two purposes:

1. It adds surface area to a tip, reducing the chance that poorly designed tip punctures our equipment, regardless of being "blunt." A 350N jacket can repel a good safety tip better than an 800N jacket can repel an untipped sword.
1. It gives us a clear visual indicator of when a tip has broken, which may not be noticeable on an untipped blade. You might think it would be easy to notice a broken tip without a visual aid but you would be wrong.

If attending tournaments, look for rulesets that require tipping. Tournaments are more likely to produce broken blades because there are more fencers, more exchanges, people are fencing at higher intensity, and most fencers do not know each other. Tournament organizers that do not require tipping are putting you unnecessarily in danger and I would recommend not attending these events.

There are several methods of tipping but I'm going to focus on one that you should not use and two that you can.

## Rubber tips

Rubber tips are fantastic for adding surface area, but tend to "stick" to masks, increasing concussion risk. Rubber tips should not be used.

## Leather tips

Leather tips wrapped with bright hockey tape are an easy and inexpensive way to tip a sword. You can buy bag of scrap leather from Michaels that will probably last you forever, and hockey tape at Sportchek. Someone at class can show you how to apply a leather tip.

## Thermoplastic tips

Thermoplastic tips can be heated up and shaped over the tip of your sword, creating a hard rounded tip that glances off an opponent's mask rather than taking it for a ride. Even the smallest bag of thermoplastic beads will probably last you forever.

Thermoplastic tips work best with rolled or spatulated sword tips, as it gives them something to "hold" when the cool and harden. It's not a bad idea to wrap these in colourful hockey tape both as a visual indicator, and to help keep them in place.
